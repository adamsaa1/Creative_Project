Part II · Systems of Exclusion

Three mechanisms that blocked educational opportunity.

These were not isolated unfair acts. They were systems that shaped where families could live, how communities could influence policy, and which schools received resources.

Taken together, they help explain why unequal education could survive even when the language of equality had entered American law.

The next section asks visitors to estimate long-run outcomes. The goal is to make visible how much inequality endured, even after many people assume the biggest barriers had already fallen.

1

Jim Crow laws

Jim Crow was the system of state and local laws that enforced racial segregation across the South. In education, it meant Black children were pushed into separate schools that were often dramatically underfunded, overcrowded, and denied the facilities, materials, and political support available to White schools.

2

Redlining

Redlining blocked many Black families from buying homes in neighborhoods with stronger tax bases and better-funded schools. Because public education was deeply tied to housing patterns and local wealth, residential exclusion translated into fewer educational opportunities and weaker long-term school funding.

3

Black disenfranchisement and voter suppression

Poll taxes, literacy tests, intimidation, and other disfranchising laws weakened Black political power across the South. That meant Black communities had far less ability to shape school boards, challenge unequal funding, or influence state education policy, allowing inequities to persist for decades.
